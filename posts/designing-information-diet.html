Designing Your Information Diet

What you consume shapes what you become. A framework for intentional consumption.

We carefully consider the food we eat. We understand that constant junk food leads to poor health, that nutrition matters, that you become what you consume in a very literal sense.

The same logic applies to information. What you read, watch, and listen to shapes your thoughts, vocabulary, mental models, and worldview. Intellectual junk food—clickbait, outrage media, low-quality content—produces intellectual malnutrition just as reliably as fast food produces physical malnutrition.

This post is a framework for designing an intentional information diet.

The Problem with Default Consumption

Without deliberate design, information consumption follows the path of least resistance: whatever's most compelling in the moment, whatever algorithms recommend, whatever everyone else is talking about.

This produces predictable results:

  • Recency bias: Consuming mostly news and current content, missing evergreen material that's survived the test of time
  • Outrage orientation: Algorithms optimize for engagement, which correlates with emotional intensity rather than insight
  • Echo chambers: Algorithmic personalization creates bubbles that confirm existing beliefs
  • Shallow processing: Skimming many things rather than deeply engaging with a few

The result is an illusion of being informed while actually becoming less capable of clear thought.

Principles for Better Information Diet

1. Prefer Books to Articles

Books represent filtered, developed thought—years of work compressed into a single artifact. Articles are often reactions, fragments, or summaries of books. Reading the source rather than summaries of the source produces deeper understanding.

This doesn't mean never reading articles. But the ratio matters. Something like 70% books, 30% articles produces better intellectual nutrition than the inverse.

2. Prefer Old to New

The Lindy Effect: what has survived longest is most likely to survive further. A book from 1960 that's still read has passed a filter that last week's bestseller hasn't faced yet.

This doesn't mean ignoring current work—new ideas do emerge. But overweighting the new at the expense of the tested is a mistake. Most hot takes cool quickly; classics remain useful.

3. Prefer Depth to Breadth

Reading one book deeply—taking notes, re-reading, applying ideas—produces more value than skimming ten books. The quality of engagement matters more than quantity of consumption.

Resist the urge to "stay current" with everything. Accept that you'll miss things. But what you actually engage becomes part of you.

4. Minimize News

Most news is noise—events that feel urgent but don't affect decisions or change your model of the world. The truly important will reach you regardless; the rest is distraction.

A weekly or even monthly news consumption habit produces similar decision-quality with dramatically less time investment. Daily news checking is almost never necessary.

5. Diversify Perspectives

Deliberately consume viewpoints you disagree with, from intelligent advocates. This isn't about "balance" in some empty sense; it's about stress-testing your own views against the best opposing arguments.

My Current Diet

What I actually do:

  • Books: 2-3 per month, actively read with notes. Mix of current interest and classics.
  • Newsletters: 5-10 carefully selected writers, read weekly. Unsubscribed from everything that consistently fails to provide value.
  • Podcasts: Long-form interviews with interesting people, during walks and chores. No reactive commentary shows.
  • Social media: Minimal. Curated follows, time-limited usage. Heavily filtered for signal.
  • News: Once weekly at most. Major events reach me through other channels.

Practical Implementation

Conduct an audit. For a week, track what you consume. Where does time actually go? The gap between ideal and actual is often shocking.

Unsubscribe ruthlessly. Email lists, podcast subscriptions, social follows—if something consistently fails to provide value, remove it. The attention it claims isn't free.

Create friction for junk. Delete apps from phone. Use website blockers. Make the low-value consumption harder to access, and the high-value consumption easier.

Schedule consumption. Rather than checking things whenever, define specific times for specific consumption types. Protected reading time produces more than scattered browsing.

Process what you consume. Take notes. Write responses. Discuss with others. Information that isn't processed rarely becomes knowledge.

"You are what you read. If you want to make better decisions, read better books."
— Shane Parrish
