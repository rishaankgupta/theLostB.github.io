Why I Read Classic Literature

In a world of infinite new content, the case for reading old books.

Every day, more content is published than anyone could consume in a lifetime. The sheer volume creates an obvious question: with so much new material available, why would anyone spend time on books written centuries ago by people long dead?

I've spent the past year reading classics deliberately—Homer, Plato, Montaigne, Dostoevsky, Austen—alongside plenty of contemporary work. The contrast clarified why classics matter in ways I hadn't expected.

The Lindy Effect

The Lindy Effect suggests that the longer something has survived, the longer it's likely to continue surviving. A book that's been read for two thousand years is more likely to be read for another hundred years than a book published last month.

This isn't mysticism—it's selection pressure. Most ideas, once expressed, are quickly forgotten. The few that persist across generations do so because they keep proving valuable to new readers in new circumstances. Time is a filter that has already done work you'd otherwise have to do yourself.

When you read Marcus Aurelius or Seneca, you're not reading work that happened to survive. You're reading work that was selected, repeatedly, by billions of readers across millennia. The odds that it contains something worthwhile are higher than any bestseller list can match.

Escape from the Present

Contemporary culture has a particular character: its assumptions, metaphors, and concerns. When you only consume current work, you absorb these assumptions unconsciously. They become invisible because they're so pervasive.

Classics come from different worlds with different assumptions. The Greeks conceived of honor differently; the Victorians conceived of duty differently; the Medievals conceived of meaning differently. Encountering these alternatives makes your own cultural assumptions visible—and thus questionable.

This isn't relativism. You don't have to adopt ancient views. But understanding that intelligent people have held very different views forces genuine reflection on why you hold yours.

The Well-Spoken Mind

Your internal monologue is composed in language. The quality and range of that language shapes what you can think. Feeding your mind on tweets and LinkedIn posts produces a certain vocabulary; feeding it on Shakespeare and Milton produces another.

Classics expand not just vocabulary but the possibilities of expression. Reading Montaigne teaches you to be a personal essayist; reading Nietzsche teaches you to be an aphorist; reading Austen teaches you to be ironic. These aren't just stylistic preferences—they're different modes of thought that become available through reading.

I notice this most when writing. Phrases and patterns absorbed from classic authors surface naturally. Reading good prose teaches you to write good prose in ways that reading about writing never can.

The Shared Inheritance

Western culture is in constant dialogue with its classics. Modern literature references, parodies, and builds on works that came before. Without knowing the originals, you miss the conversation.

When you read the Odyssey, you suddenly understand references in work from Dante to Joyce to Coen Brothers films. The Bible, Shakespeare, Greek mythology—these aren't arbitrary reading lists but central nodes in a cultural network. Knowing them makes everything else richer.

This applies far beyond literature. Political philosophy engages Plato, Locke, Rousseau. Modern ethics engages Aristotle and Kant. Science engages Newton, Darwin, Einstein. The thinkers who shaped current discourse are still worth knowing directly.

Slower Reading

Contemporary content is optimized for easy consumption. Classics often aren't. They require patience, re-reading, and effort that modern media conditions us against.

This difficulty is a feature, not a bug. Struggling with a text engages you in ways that frictionless consumption doesn't. You can't skim Kant or speedread Dostoevsky—you have to actually think.

The effort of reading classics trains a particular cognitive capacity: the ability to grapple with difficult ideas over extended periods. This transfers beyond books to any complex problem that can't be understood quickly.

How to Start

If classics seem daunting, some approaches make them more accessible:

  • Start with translations that read well. A good modern translation of Homer beats a clunky older one.
  • Read alongside commentary. Introductions and reading guides provide context that makes old texts more accessible.
  • Begin with shorter works. Seneca's letters, Montaigne's essays, short dialogues of Plato—you don't have to start with War and Peace.
  • Follow interest. The classics that resonate vary by person. Read widely enough to find your own canon.
  • Reread. Classics reveal new layers on rereading. A book you read at sixteen hits differently at twenty, and will again at forty.

The Objection

"But the world has changed. What can dead people teach us about modern problems?"

More than you'd expect. Technology changes rapidly; human nature changes slowly. The psychology Dostoevsky depicted, the ethics Aristotle analyzed, the political dynamics Thucydides observed—these remain relevant because the humans involved haven't changed much.

Contemporary thinkers are often more useful for understanding specific current situations. But classics address perennial questions that current situations exemplify. Both perspectives are valuable; neither is complete.

"The great books are the books that never have to be written again."
— Mortimer Adler
