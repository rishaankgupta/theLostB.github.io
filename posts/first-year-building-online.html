My First Year Building Online

An honest retrospective. The wins, the failures, the numbers, and the lessons that actually mattered.

One year ago, I published my first real piece of writing online. Not a school assignment, not a private journal entry—something intended for strangers to read and judge. The experience was terrifying.

This is an honest accounting of what happened next. Not a highlight reel, not false modesty, but the actual story with the numbers included. If you're considering building something online, this might be useful.

Let me contextualize these numbers. 47 posts in 12 months means roughly one per week, with some variation. 12K total readers means an average of about 250 readers per post—not viral, but not invisible either. 3.2K newsletter subscribers accumulated gradually, with about 40% open rates. And zero revenue was intentional: I wanted to build an audience before monetizing.

Are these good numbers? Honestly, I don't know. I've seen people achieve more in less time. I've also seen people quit after posting to silence for months. Comparison is treacherous because outcomes depend enormously on niche, timing, and luck.

What Actually Worked

1. Consistency Over Perfection

My first posts are embarrassing in hindsight. The writing was clumsy, the ideas half-formed, the formatting amateurish. But publishing regularly—even when I wasn't ready—built the habit that everything else depends on. The alternative was waiting until I was "good enough," which means waiting forever.

2. Going Deep on Specific Topics

My best-performing posts weren't broad overviews. They were deep dives into specific questions. "17 Ways to Learn Faster" outperformed "Thoughts on Education" by 10x. Specificity signals expertise and provides immediately useful content.

3. Cross-Pollination

Being a "polymath" sounds pretentious, but connecting ideas from different fields turned out to be genuinely valuable. Posts that bridged philosophy and technology, or science and creativity, attracted readers who cared about both domains. The intersection is less crowded than the mainstream.

4. Investing in Design

The brutalist aesthetic of this site wasn't accidental. It communicates something about the content before anyone reads a word. Visual distinctiveness helps people remember you in an endless scroll of sameness.

What Didn't Work

1. Twitter/X Strategy (Initially)

I spent months trying to "build in public" on Twitter. Posted threads, engaged with hashtags, followed the playbook. Results: almost nothing. The platform rewards controversy and takes rather than depth and nuance. I eventually found a more modest use—sharing posts and connecting with readers—but abandoned the "growth strategy" approach entirely.

2. Trying to Write Like Others

I have an embarrassing folder of posts where I tried to imitate successful writers. The imitations were worse than my authentic attempts, and readers could tell. Finding your voice takes time you can't shortcut by copying someone else's.

3. Optimizing Before Creating

I spent weeks on SEO research, social media strategy, and growth hacking tactics before I had a body of work worth finding. Premature optimization. The basics—write consistently, write well enough, share genuinely—matter more than tactics for a long time.

The Real Lessons

Beyond tactics, here's what the year actually taught me:

The resistance never goes away. Every single post still feels scary before publishing. The fear doesn't disappear with experience; you just get better at acting despite it.

Most feedback is noise. Likes and shares correlate weakly with actual impact. The posts that changed how people thought often got less engagement than clever takes. Learn to distinguish signal from noise.

Delayed gratification is real. Posts I thought flopped sometimes found readers months later through search. The internet has a long tail if you're patient.

The network compounds. Early on, every reader felt hard-won. Later, existing readers brought new readers. The snowball effect takes a while to start, but then accelerates.

Writing clarifies thinking. The private benefit of writing—organizing my own thoughts—exceeded the public benefit of reaching readers. Even if no one read this, the process would be valuable.

Year Two

Looking forward, I'm planning to:

  • Increase publishing frequency (aiming for 75 posts in Year Two)
  • Introduce paid content once free content has proven its worth
  • Collaborate more with other writers and thinkers
  • Build tools and resources beyond pure writing
  • Continue going deeper on topics rather than broader

If you're starting your own building-online journey, here's my honest advice: expect month one to feel pointless, months three through six to feel discouraging, and only around month nine or ten to start seeing compound effects. Most people quit in the discouraging middle. If you can persist through it, you'll find yourself in surprisingly uncrowded territory.

"The best time to start was a year ago. The second best time is now."
