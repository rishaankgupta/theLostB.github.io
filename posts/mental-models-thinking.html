Mental Models That Changed My Thinking

The frameworks that most improved my decision-making. Moving from instinct to intentional reasoning.

Mental models are thinking tools—frameworks you can apply across domains to understand situations and make decisions. The more models you have, the more ways you can analyze problems. A carpenter with only a hammer sees every problem as a nail; a thinker with many models can match tool to problem.

These are the models that most changed how I think. Not an exhaustive catalog, but a personal canon of frameworks that have proven useful repeatedly.

1. Inversion

Instead of asking "How do I succeed?" ask "How would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things.

Inversion cuts through complexity. When I can't figure out what to do, I can usually figure out what would definitely be wrong. The answer often becomes obvious once I list the failure modes.

Applying it: Before a new project, list everything that would make it fail. Before a conversation, list everything that would make it go badly. The inverted perspective reveals risks you wouldn't see directly.

2. Second-Order Thinking

Ask "And then what?" beyond the immediate consequences. Most decisions have effects that cascade.

First-order thinkers see one move ahead. Second-order thinkers trace the chain of consequences. A policy that sounds great might have second-order effects that make it terrible; an investment that sounds bad might have second-order benefits that make it brilliant.

Applying it: For any decision, explicitly trace at least two levels of consequences. What happens next? And after that? The best decisions look good at multiple levels.

3. Circle of Competence

Know what you actually understand versus what you think you understand. Stay within the former.

Everyone has domains where they have genuine expertise and domains where they're operating on vibes. The key is knowing which is which. Those who confuse the two make costly mistakes; those who stay in their circle—while working to expand it—make fewer errors.

Applying it: For important decisions, honestly assess: Am I within my circle of competence? If not, either get help from someone who is, or invest in understanding before acting.

4. Opportunity Cost

The true cost of something is not just what you pay, but what you give up by choosing it.

Every choice forecloses other choices. The college you attend means not attending others. The book you read means not reading another. This seems obvious, but we routinely fail to account for it, evaluating options in isolation rather than against alternatives.

Applying it: For any significant commitment, ask: What else could I do with this time/money/energy? Is this the best possible use, or just better than nothing?

5. Hanlon's Razor

Never attribute to malice what can be adequately explained by stupidity (or ignorance, or accident).

When someone wrongs you, the instinct is to assume they acted intentionally. Usually they just weren't thinking. Assuming malice creates conflict where none was required; assuming accident preserves relationships while still allowing you to address the problem.

Applying it: When something goes wrong with other people involved, start with the assumption of incompetence or accident, not malice. You can always update toward malice if evidence requires, but starting there rarely helps.

6. Maps and Territory

The map is not the territory. Models represent reality; they are not reality itself.

Every model, framework, or theory simplifies some aspects of reality to make others tractable. The usefulness of a model depends on context—a model perfect for one purpose may be useless for another. Confusing the model with reality leads to applying it where it doesn't fit.

Applying it: For any model you use (including these), remember what it leaves out. When the model's predictions mismatch reality, update the model, not your perception of reality.

7. Ergodicity

What works on average doesn't always work for individuals over time. Avoid risks that can eliminate you.

Russian roulette has positive expected value (5/6 chance of winning the pot, 1/6 chance of death). But you can't play enough rounds for the average to manifest—the 1/6 outcome ends your game. Many risks work this way: favorable on average, but catastrophic for any individual who draws the wrong number.

Applying it: Identify risks with catastrophic downside (ruin, death, irreversibility) and avoid them regardless of expected value. Never risk what you can't afford to lose.

8. Comparative Advantage

Do what you do relatively better, even if others do it absolutely better. Trade completes the picture.

You don't need to be the best at something for it to be worth doing. You need to be relatively better at it compared to your other options, while others are relatively better at what they do. Specialization and trade beat trying to do everything yourself.

Applying it: Focus on what you do comparatively well; outsource or trade for what others do comparatively better. Even if you're better at both tasks, your time is better spent on your strongest area.

Using Models Together

The power of mental models increases with quantity. A single model gives one perspective; multiple models give triangulation. When several models converge on the same answer, you can have more confidence. When they diverge, you know to investigate further.

Charlie Munger calls this "latticework of mental models"—the interconnected framework that supports clearer thinking. Building it takes years. Reading widely across disciplines. Studying how experts in different fields think. Practicing application until models become reflexive.

Start with a few. Apply them deliberately until they become automatic. Add more over time. The investment compounds—each additional model makes all the others more useful.

"I think it is undeniably true that the human brain must work in models. The trick is to have your brain work better than the other person's brain because it understands the most fundamental models."
— Charlie Munger
